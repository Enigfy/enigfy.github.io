Marienbad

Strategy game based on the mathematical game of Nim.

Marienbad is a beautiful, strategic cat-stacking game based on misère Nim (the version where the last move loses). It’s easy to learn but deeply rewarding once you understand the hidden math.

The Game Rules

The board always starts with four columns of cats:

1 • 3 • 5 • 7

Players take turns.

On your turn you must remove one or more cats from a single column only.

Cats are always taken from the top down of that column.

Crucial rule: The player who is forced to take the very last cat on the entire board loses.

Your goal is to leave exactly one cat for your opponent.

How to move

Press and hold any cat, then swipe up or sideways (left or right). Everything from the cat you touched upward will fly off the screen with a satisfying animation.

The computer always plays perfectly (it uses the optimal Nim strategy with the special misère adjustment for the endgame).

History

Origins

The history of Nim, a classic impartial combinatorial game in which two players alternate removing objects from one or more distinct heaps, with the player who takes the last object typically winning (or losing, in misère variants), has conceptual origins in ancient practices of manipulating counters, although direct evidence for the precise multi-heap form is limited.

In prehistoric times, early humans likely engaged in rudimentary strategic activities using stones, seeds, bones, or other small objects arranged in piles. Such manipulations may have served recreational, divinatory, or decision-making purposes across various societies. These practices represent conceptual precursors to subtraction or takeaway games, sharing similarities with the broader family of pebble-distribution games that later evolved in different cultures.

Ancient China

The game is traditionally linked to ancient China, where variants of stone-removal games, sometimes referred to as jiǎn-shízǐ ("picking stones"), are believed to have circulated for many centuries, possibly predating written records. These early forms emphasized strategic removal under constraints and contributed to the foundational ideas underlying modern Nim.

European Renaissance

European references to games resembling Nim appear in the early 16th century. A notable example is found in the manuscript De Viribus Quantitatis (c. 1508) by the Italian mathematician Luca Pacioli, which describes a simplified single-pile variant involving the addition or removal of numbers up to a fixed limit to reach a target. Similar recreations circulated in Renaissance mathematical literature.

Modern Times

The modern multi-heap version of Nim received formal mathematical recognition in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. In 1901, Harvard mathematician Charles Leonard Bouton published his seminal paper "Nim, A Game with a Complete Mathematical Theory," in which he introduced the Nim-sum (the bitwise XOR of heap sizes) as the key to determining winning and losing positions. Bouton is credited with naming the game "Nim," possibly derived from the German imperative nimm ("take").

Subsequent developments in the 20th century included early computing demonstrations, such as the Nimatron (1940), an electromechanical device capable of playing perfect Nim, as well as digital implementations.

Marienbad

In 1961, the game gained wider cultural prominence through its prominent appearance in Alain Resnais's avant-garde film L'Année dernière à Marienbad (Last Year at Marienbad). In the film, characters repeatedly play a variant of Nim using matchsticks arranged in four rows (typically of 1, 3, 5, and 7 objects), with the player forced to take the last object losing. This cinematic depiction, in which one character consistently defeats another through superior strategy, introduced the game to a larger audience.

Variants with modified rules or misère conditions continue to be studied in combinatorial game theory.

In summary, Nim is a very ancient place where implacable mathematical logic meets our understanding of how we think.
